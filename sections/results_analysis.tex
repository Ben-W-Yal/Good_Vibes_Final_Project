\section{Results and Analysis}

\subsection{What we observe qualitatively}

In internal runs during development, GeoIntel reliably delivers four coupled outcomes end-to-end: \textbf{(i)} users can relocate quickly from headline-scale curiosity to map-scale geography; \textbf{(ii)} PDB sections tend to arrive as structured narratives with accompanying citations when Gemini~\cite{gemini-api} grounding succeeds; \textbf{(iii)} optional ion-backed terrain and OSM-style building meshes~\cite{cesium}, when enabled, give terrain-rich theaters recognizable vertical structure beyond satellite imagery alone; and \textbf{(iv)} per-event panels surface streamed analyst summaries plus conversational Q\&A tied to the selected record's context. PDF export remains available for slide-shaped classroom deliverables when a document artifact is preferable to screen capture.
Tables~\ref{tab:rubric} and~\ref{tab:rubric-scores} add a small numeric complement: an internal rubric and mean teammate scores on two archived outputs, cross-checking these qualitative claims without overstating statistical power.

Formal benchmarks were out of scope for this course delivery; claims below are qualitative unless noted.

\paragraph{Social-amplified and noisy events in practice.}
Many GlobeEntity rows ultimately trace to social virality or rapid-fire posting (e.g., protest hashtags, strike footage, conflicting early counts) before wire services converge. In one recurring demo pattern, a spike of GDELT-style rows on unrest in a capital appears alongside contradictory headlines; the AI analyst summary and chat are instructed to hedge on unverified casualties, while the PDB section (with grounding) can surface multiple URLs for triage. The UI does not ``solve'' disinformation; it concentrates geography, timestamps, and explicit citations so a human can see \emph{where} rumor clusters before accepting a single line of narrative.

\subsection{Strengths}

\textbf{Integrated situational picture:} Combining feeds and trackers with terrain-aware navigation reduces context switching versus opening separate dashboards.

\textbf{Grounded summaries when healthy:} Gemini~\cite{gemini-api} outputs tied to citations improve inspectability versus unconstrained chat answers. Per-event chat inherits the same caution: answers are only as good as the normalized event fields and related URLs supplied in context.

\textbf{Explicit operational boundaries:} Section-by-section generation surfaces failures locally instead of collapsing an entire deck silently.

\subsection{Limitations and brittle behavior}

\textbf{Upstream variability:} GDELT-like endpoints~\cite{gdelt} can throttle or return sparse windows; AIS feeds depend on reception patterns and credential tiers.

\textbf{Model behavior:} Grounded briefings occasionally truncate JSON-shaped outputs under token limits or overload responses; citation counts fluctuate by topic.
In one debugging session, an event chat draft briefly over-attributed an air strike to a state actor when the underlying feed card only contained ``alleged'' language from a secondary tabloid; the error was visible after expanding the primary URL in the sidebar, and tightening chat instructions to quote the event \texttt{description} field reduced recurrence.

\textbf{Imagery extraction:} Article imagery depends on publisher markup (Open Graph/Twitter tags); many pages yield no embeddable preview image even when reporting is solid.

\textbf{Geolocation realism:} Automated coordinates can default to coarse regions when upstream feeds omit precise lat/lon.

\subsection{Informal evaluation}

We exercised representative workflows during integration testing (single-machine demos): globe navigation, feed toggling, per-event summaries and chat, PDB generation under configured Gemini~\cite{gemini-api} keys, and PDF export.
No structured human-subjects study was conducted; informal consensus among teammates emphasized speed-to-context as the strongest perceived gain versus bookmark tabs alone.
\textbf{External participants:} the project did not include a formal usability session with non-teammate participants under IRB-style consent. A natural extension would be timed tasks with several external volunteers (locate an event, generate one PDB section, ask two chat questions) with short structured debriefs. The numeric entries in Table~\ref{tab:rubric-scores} are therefore \textbf{internal team scores only}.

\subsection{Rubric-based self-assessment (lightweight)}

To go one step beyond purely qualitative impressions, all six teammates independently scored \textbf{two} artifacts from the same integration week: \textbf{(A)} one PDB \emph{Executive Summary} section with visible citations, and \textbf{(B)} one event thread (auto summary plus two follow-up chat turns). Each person assigned integer scores from 1 (poor) to 5 (strong) on four dimensions in Table~\ref{tab:rubric}. We then averaged scores per artifact and dimension; parentheses show the sample standard deviation across raters ($n=6$), which reflects disagreement, not statistical power.

\textbf{Protocol notes:} raters had full access to the running app and source URLs shown in the UI; there was no blinding to authorship and no external panel. The rubric documents internal team judgment under course time constraints and is not presented as independent external validation.

\textbf{Interpretation:} Artifact (A) peaks on \textbf{D2} (structure), consistent with PDB slide templates and section headers. Artifact (B) trails slightly on \textbf{D1} (source anchoring), matching the absence of live web grounding for event chat---the model must lean on the normalized card and related URLs, so raters penalize looser attribution when chatter drifts from those fields. \subsection{Operating economics (order-of-magnitude)}

Order-of-magnitude figures anchor variable costs (retail API pricing shifts; treat as April~2026 planning estimates):

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Gemini usage:} A full multi-section PDB with search grounding~\cite{gemini-api} can consume on the order of \textbf{tens of thousands} of output tokens plus tool-assisted calls---often \textbf{well under one U.S.\ dollar} per complete brief in our development configuration, though retries or verbose sections move the needle. A single per-event summary plus a short chat thread is typically \textbf{cents}, not dollars.
  \item \textbf{Third-party trackers/maps:} Optional licensed trackers (where configured) carry vendor-specific metering; Cesium ion terrain/buildings~\cite{cesium} uses project quotas. Classroom demos on free tiers rarely hit hard limits.
  \item \textbf{Hosting footprint:} Local development requires modest CPU/RAM; production deployment cost depends on provider choice and was not exercised here.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Hypothetical pricing sketch:} As a SaaS analyst workstation for small teams, a \textbf{tiered monthly subscription} (base seats + monthly PDB generation caps + optional premium tracker add-ons) would align recurring revenue with Gemini and data-vendor marginal cost and discourage abusive polling.

\subsection{Competitive landscape}

\textbf{News-only aggregators} (e.g., \emph{Google News}-style headline rivers) optimize breadth but rarely fuse spatial trackers with API-backed, citation-grounded intelligence sections in one workspace.

\textbf{Standalone globe tools} (e.g., \emph{Google Earth}-class viewers) visualize geography without reproducible ingest adapters, structured PDB workflows, and explicit separation between grounded and context-only model calls.

\textbf{Consumer chatbots} (e.g., \emph{ChatGPT}-style conversational UIs) answer ad hoc questions yet omit persistent map-linked event state, server-side adapter discipline, and the event-scoped chat pattern implemented here.

GeoIntel differentiates through \textbf{(i)} adapter-backed ingestion under explicit caching discipline and \textbf{(ii)} coupling Gemini-grounded PDB outputs~\cite{gemini-api} with terrain-centric exploration~\cite{cesium} rather than chat-only summaries.
